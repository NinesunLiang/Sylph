     7|## 1. To Everyone Who Has Been Hurt by AI
     8|
     9|You have experienced this moment.
    10|
    11|Late at night. You finally explained the requirements clearly. AI confidently says "it is done." You open the file with anticipation — nothing has changed. Or worse, it has changed, but in the wrong way. You do not get angry. You re-explain, try again. AI says: "OK, I understand." And it is wrong again.
    12|
    13|Or you have been working for three hours. The conversation is getting longer. AI starts forgetting the rules you stated in your very first message. It starts writing code in the way you explicitly said "do not use." You correct it. It apologizes, changes it, then makes the same mistake in the next file. You realize you are not working with a reliable collaborator. You are **babysitting** something that is forgetful, that lies, that is sometimes brilliant but cannot be trusted.
    14|
    15|Or one day you mentioned the path to your `.env` file in the conversation. Just one sentence. Then you start worrying. Did it read it? Did it remember it? Are these keys still safe? No one tells you the answer.
    16|
    17|Or you spent two days having AI help you build a proposal. It generated beautiful documentation with data, charts, percentages. You were excited. You shared it with others. Then someone asks: "What is the source of this data?" You look for it. You cannot find it. Because those numbers were fabricated by AI.
    18|
    19|**None of this is your fault.**
    20|This is a real structural problem:
    21|
    22|> **AI is powerful, but it does not care about the consequences. It produces its output, and its responsibility ends there. You bear the consequences.**
    23|
    24|And you are one person. You have real work to deliver, real deadlines, real losses. What you need is not a smarter AI. **What you need is someone watching over it for you.**

    28|## 2. The Most Invisible Source of Fatigue in the AI Era: Cognitive Overload
    29|
    30|You are more tired. This is not a contradiction. Because **the increase in output and the increase in mental burden are simultaneous — and the latter grows faster.**
    31|
    32|### Layer 1: The Sense of Unknown Danger
    33|
    34|Every time AI outputs something, you are uncertain: Is this true? Is this complete? Are there errors I have not found? Did it do something I do not know about?
    35|
    36|This uncertainty is not occasional anxiety. It is **continuous background noise.** Like driving on a road you do not know — not tense, but your nervous system is in a state of mild alert. Two hours later, you get out of the car and find yourself more tired than usual, without being able to explain why. You did not do anything particularly draining. But you were **monitoring** the whole time.
    37|
    38|**Monitoring is invisible manual labor.**
    39|
    40|### Layer 2: The Sense of Losing Control
    41|
    42|Before, when you wrote every line of code, you knew what it did. Now AI generates five hundred lines and you can only skim them. The output is yours, the responsibility is yours, but you did not fully participate in the process. This is not laziness. It is **structural loss of control.** Humans have a natural discomfort with things they do not fully understand but are responsible for. This discomfort drains you continuously, because problems can **happen at any time.**
    43|
    44|### Layer 3: The Helplessness of Information Overload
    45|
    46|AI can output in one second what takes you ten minutes to read and verify. You cannot keep up. So you compromise — you glance at it, it looks about right, you accept it. But the act of judging "about right" itself consumes you. Because you know you have not truly verified. You have simply **chosen to trust.** And choosing to trust something you know is not fully trustworthy requires actively suppressing your instinct to doubt. Suppressing instinct is extremely energy-intensive.
    47|
    48|### Layer 4: The Sense of Role Displacement
    49|
    50|You thought using AI would let you do more valuable work. But in reality, you spend a huge amount of time:
    51|- Correcting AI's mistakes
    52|- Re-explaining rules AI has forgotten
    53|- Verifying the truthfulness of AI's outputs
    54|- Dealing with new problems AI introduces
    55|- Managing AI's behavioral boundaries
    56|
    57|None of this is the work you wanted to do. This is **babysitting work.** You have become AI's caretaker, not its collaborator. You are using the most advanced tool, yet feeling like you are doing the most inefficient work.
    58|
    59|### The Name of This Fatigue
    60|
    61|Cognitive Overload. But not ordinary information overload. It is a special kind, **sustained by uncertainty.** Ordinary overload is having too much information to process. AI-era overload is: **you processed the information, but you do not know if your processing was correct.** This "not knowing" makes all completed work feel incomplete. It makes all resolved problems feel unresolved. **You keep working, but you never truly put things down.**

   127|## 6. Editorial Feedback Notes
   128|
   129|### On the Use of "Everyone" and "Every"
   130|
   131|Titles and dedications may use "every," but try not to use "all" or "everyone" in the body text. Because:
   132|
   133|1. **Reduces risk of overgeneralization**: "Everyone" is too absolute and easily refuted. Once readers start arguing about the quantifier, their attention drifts from your core message.
   134|2. **Aligns with brand tone**: Carror OS's tone is sincere, restrained — no sloganeering, no exaggeration. "Most people," "many people," "more and more people" fit the overall style better than "everyone."
   135|3. **More credible**: Changing "everyone" to "most people" does not weaken the message — it strengthens credibility. Readers feel you are not speaking for everyone, but earnestly describing a widely shared reality without forcing universality.
   136|
